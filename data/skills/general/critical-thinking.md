# Critical Thinking

Evaluate arguments, recognize biases, and form well-reasoned conclusions.

## Logical Fallacies to Recognize

### Most Common in Business/Tech
| Fallacy | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Appeal to authority | "X is true because expert says so" | "The CEO wants it, so it must be right" |
| False dichotomy | Presenting only 2 options when more exist | "Either we build it in-house or we fail" |
| Sunk cost | Continuing because of past investment | "We've spent 6 months on this, we can't stop now" |
| Survivorship bias | Only looking at successes, ignoring failures | "Successful startups all did X" (ignoring failures that also did X) |
| Correlation ≠ causation | Assuming cause from correlation | "We launched feature X and revenue grew" (other factors?) |
| Appeal to novelty | New = better | "AI can solve this" without evaluating if simpler solutions work |
| Slippery slope | Assuming extreme consequences without evidence | "If we allow this exception, everything will break down" |

## Cognitive Biases

### Decision-Making Biases
- **Confirmation bias**: Seeking information that confirms what you already believe
  - Counter: Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Ask "What would change my mind?"
- **Anchoring bias**: Over-relying on the first piece of information
  - Counter: Generate your own estimate before hearing others
- **Availability bias**: Overweighting recent or vivid events
  - Counter: Look at base rates and statistical data, not just memorable examples
- **Dunning-Kruger effect**: Overconfidence when you know little about a topic
  - Counter: Seek feedback from domain experts, calibrate with data

### Group Biases
- **Groupthink**: Desire for harmony overrides critical evaluation
  - Counter: Assign a devil's advocate role, use anonymous input
- **Bandwagon effect**: Believing something because many others do
  - Counter: Evaluate evidence independently before discussing with the group
- **Authority bias**: Deferring to hierarchy instead of evidence
  - Counter: Evaluate arguments on their merits, not their source

## Argument Evaluation

### Steel-Manning
- Before criticizing an argument, state it in its strongest possible form
- "The best version of your argument is..." then respond to that version
- Forces genuine engagement with ideas (not just attacking weak versions)
- Builds trust and often reveals where the real disagreement lies

### Evaluating Evidence
1. **Source**: Is it credible? Any conflicts of interest?
2. **Sample size**: One anecdote ≠ evidence. How large is N?
3. **Methodology**: How was this data collected? Any systematic errors?
4. **Reproducibility**: Has anyone independently verified this?
5. **Recency**: Is this evidence still current, or has the situation changed?

### Argument Structure
- **Claim**: What is being asserted?
- **Evidence**: What facts support the claim?
- **Reasoning**: How does the evidence lead to the claim?
- **Assumptions**: What must be true for the reasoning to hold?
- **Counter-evidence**: What evidence contradicts the claim?

## Practical Application

### Before Making a Decision
- What is my prior belief? Am I biased toward a specific outcome?
- What would a smart person who disagrees with me say?
- What evidence would change my mind? (If nothing would, I'm not thinking critically)
- Am I confusing "I want this to be true" with "this is true"?

### Red Flags in Arguments
- Emotional language instead of evidence
- Cherry-picked data (where's the full picture?)
- Unfalsifiable claims (can never be proven wrong)
- Vague claims ("studies show..." — which studies?)
- Ad hominem attacks instead of addressing the argument
