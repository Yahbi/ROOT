# Time Management

Allocate your most limited resource -- time and attention -- to the work that matters most.

## Deep Work Blocks

### Scheduling Deep Work
```
Mon-Fri Weekly Template:
  06:00-08:00  Deep work block 1 (most important task, no meetings)
  08:00-08:30  Break + email/Slack scan
  08:30-10:00  Deep work block 2 (second priority task)
  10:00-12:00  Meetings, collaboration, admin
  12:00-13:00  Lunch (actual break, not working lunch)
  13:00-14:30  Deep work block 3 (if energy allows)
  14:30-16:00  Shallow work: reviews, email, Slack, planning
  16:00-16:30  Daily shutdown: plan tomorrow's deep work blocks
```

### Deep Work Rules
- Minimum block size: 90 minutes (it takes 15-25 minutes to reach deep focus)
- No notifications: phone on silent, Slack closed, email closed
- Single task: if you switch tasks, the block is over
- Protect fiercely: treat deep work like an immovable meeting with your most important client
- Track hours: aim for 3-4 hours of actual deep work per day (most people achieve 1-2)

### Context Switching Cost
- Every interruption costs 15-25 minutes of recovery time (Gloria Mark, UC Irvine)
- 4 interruptions per hour = 0 productive minutes per hour
- A developer interrupted every 15 minutes produces 50% less quality work
- Solution: batch interruptions. Check Slack at 10:00, 12:00, 14:00 — not continuously

## Eisenhower Matrix

### Quadrant Framework
| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|--|--------|-----------|
| **Important** | Q1: DO immediately (production outage, deadline today) | Q2: SCHEDULE it (strategy, learning, relationships, health) |
| **Not Important** | Q3: DELEGATE or batch (most emails, some meetings, routine requests) | Q4: ELIMINATE (mindless browsing, unnecessary meetings, busywork) |

### Application Rules
- Most of your scheduled time should be in Q2 (important, not urgent)
- If you spend all day in Q1, you are firefighting — fix the root cause
- Q3 traps: things that feel urgent because someone else made them urgent
- Q4 audit: list everything you did yesterday and tag each item. Eliminate Q4 ruthlessly

### Weekly Planning with Eisenhower
1. List all tasks and obligations for the week
2. Assign each to a quadrant
3. Schedule Q2 items first (they are most likely to be skipped without scheduling)
4. Block time for predictable Q1 items (on-call, known deadlines)
5. Batch Q3 items into 1-2 time slots per day
6. Delete or defer Q4 items

## Energy Management

### Chronotype-Aware Scheduling
| Energy Level | Best For | Example Time |
|-------------|---------|-------------|
| Peak (highest focus) | Deep work, complex decisions, creative thinking | Morning for most, evening for night owls |
| Trough (lowest energy) | Routine admin, email, meetings | Early afternoon for most |
| Recovery (rebounding) | Creative brainstorming, open-ended exploration | Late afternoon for most |

### Energy Audit
Track energy levels (1-5) every 2 hours for 1 week. Patterns will emerge:
- When are you sharpest? (Schedule hardest work here)
- When do you slump? (Schedule low-stakes work here)
- What activities drain you? (Minimize or delegate)
- What activities energize you? (Do more of these, especially before draining tasks)

### Recovery Practices
- Take a real break every 90 minutes (walk, stretch, not phone scrolling)
- Lunch away from desk (eating while working is not a break)
- 20-minute nap at the trough if possible (proven to restore alertness)
- End the day with a shutdown ritual (plan tomorrow, close all tabs, physically leave workspace)

## Saying No

### Why Saying No Is Critical
- Every yes is a no to something else (your time is finite)
- The most productive people say no to almost everything (Warren Buffett's "20-slot" rule)
- An enthusiastic yes or a firm no — "maybe" and "I'll try" lead to overcommitment

### How to Say No Effectively
| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Meeting invite with no agenda | "Happy to help — could you share the agenda and what you need from me? I want to make sure I'm the right person" |
| Scope expansion on your project | "I can do X or Y but not both in this sprint. Which is higher priority?" |
| Favor that conflicts with deep work | "I can't help today but I'm free Thursday afternoon. Would that work?" |
| Task outside your responsibility | "That's outside my area — [name] would be better suited. I can introduce you" |

### The "Hell Yes or No" Framework
- If a request does not make you think "this is exactly what I should be doing," decline
- Applied to commitments: if you would not accept it if it were happening tomorrow, do not accept it for next month
- Protect your calendar like you protect your codebase: no unauthorized changes

## Weekly Review (30 Minutes, Friday)

### Review Checklist
1. **Completed**: What did I accomplish this week? (Acknowledge progress)
2. **Incomplete**: What did I not finish? (Reschedule or drop)
3. **Lessons**: What worked? What did not? (Adjust approach)
4. **Next week**: What are the 3 most important outcomes for next week?
5. **Calendar audit**: Cancel or decline meetings that are not serving you
6. **Energy check**: Am I trending toward burnout? What needs to change?
