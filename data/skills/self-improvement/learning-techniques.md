# Learning Techniques

Learn faster and retain more using evidence-based study methods.

## Active Recall

### The Core Principle
- Passive review (re-reading, highlighting) creates familiarity, not knowledge
- Active recall (retrieving from memory) strengthens neural pathways
- Testing yourself is 3-4x more effective than re-reading the same material

### Methods
| Technique | How It Works | Best For |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Flashcards | Read question, attempt answer, check | Facts, definitions, formulas |
| Practice problems | Solve without looking at solution | Procedures, math, coding |
| Teach it | Explain concept to someone else | Deep understanding |
| Free recall | Close book, write everything you remember | Comprehensive review |
| Past exams | Simulate real conditions | Exam preparation |

## Feynman Technique

### Steps
1. **Choose a concept**: Pick something you want to understand deeply
2. **Explain it simply**: Write an explanation as if teaching a 12-year-old
3. **Identify gaps**: Where did you struggle, get vague, or use jargon?
4. **Go back to source**: Study the gaps, then try explaining again
5. **Simplify and analogize**: Use analogies and simple language until it's crystal clear

### Why It Works
- Forces you to confront what you don't actually understand
- Jargon masks confusion — simple language requires genuine understanding
- The "illusion of understanding" breaks when you try to explain from scratch
- If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough

## Spaced Repetition

### Optimal Review Schedule
- First review: 1 day after initial learning
- Second review: 3 days after first review
- Third review: 7 days later
- Fourth review: 14 days later
- Fifth review: 30 days later
- Each successful recall extends the next interval

### Implementation
- Use Anki for systematic spaced repetition
- Create cards as you learn (not in a separate batch — context matters)
- Daily review habit: 15-20 minutes, ideally same time every day
- Don't skip days — the algorithm depends on consistent review

## Interleaving

### What It Is
- Mix different topics or problem types during practice sessions
- Instead of: 20 problems of type A, then 20 of type B
- Do: A, B, C, A, C, B, A, B... randomly mixed

### Why It Works
- Forces your brain to identify which strategy applies (discrimination practice)
- Mimics real-world conditions (you don't know which type of problem you'll face)
- Feels harder during practice but produces 25-40% better retention
- Particularly effective for math, coding, and any skill with multiple techniques

## Deliberate Practice

### Components
1. **Specific goal**: Not "practice guitar" but "play measures 12-16 at 120 BPM without errors"
2. **Focus on weaknesses**: Practice what you're bad at, not what you enjoy
3. **Immediate feedback**: Know right away if you got it right or wrong
4. **Push beyond comfort**: Work at the edge of your ability (not too easy, not impossible)
5. **Reflection**: After each session, identify what improved and what still needs work

### Time Allocation
- 80% of practice time on weaknesses, 20% on maintaining strengths
- Quality > quantity: 1 hour of focused deliberate practice > 4 hours of mindless repetition
- Rest between sessions: sleep consolidates learning (don't cram)

## Learning Plan Template

1. Define the skill or knowledge area
2. Break it into sub-skills (what are the components?)
3. Prioritize: which sub-skill gives the most leverage?
4. Set measurable milestones (not "learn Python" but "build a CLI tool that does X")
5. Schedule daily practice sessions (minimum 30 minutes)
6. Review progress weekly, adjust approach based on what's working
