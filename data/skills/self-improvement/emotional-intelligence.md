# Emotional Intelligence

Develop the interpersonal skills that determine effectiveness in leadership, collaboration, and decision-making.

## Self-Awareness

### Recognizing Emotional Triggers
| Trigger | Physical Signal | Mental Signal | Healthy Response |
|---------|----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Criticism | Chest tightening, flushing | Defensive thoughts, justification | Pause. Ask: "What can I learn from this?" |
| Uncertainty | Restlessness, rapid heartbeat | Catastrophizing, analysis paralysis | Name the fear. Identify what you can control |
| Overload | Shallow breathing, tension | Scattered thinking, irritability | Step away for 10 minutes. Prioritize ruthlessly |
| Conflict | Stomach knot, jaw clenching | Blame, withdrawal | Acknowledge the emotion before responding |

### Emotional Labeling
- Name the specific emotion (not "I feel bad" but "I feel frustrated because my suggestion was dismissed")
- Granularity matters: anxious vs overwhelmed vs uncertain are different states requiring different responses
- Labeling an emotion reduces its intensity (research: "affect labeling" dampens amygdala activation)
- Journal regularly: 5 minutes at end of day, note strongest emotion and what triggered it

### Self-Assessment Questions
- What situations consistently trigger a strong emotional reaction in me?
- When do I make my worst decisions? (fatigue, anger, pressure?)
- What is my default conflict response? (fight, flight, freeze, fawn?)
- How do others experience me when I am stressed?

## Empathy

### Three Types of Empathy
| Type | Description | Application |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Cognitive | Understanding another's perspective intellectually | Code reviews, negotiations, product design |
| Emotional | Feeling what another person feels | 1-on-1s, supporting struggling teammates |
| Compassionate | Understanding + feeling + moving to help | Mentoring, leadership, conflict resolution |

### Active Listening Practices
1. **Full attention**: Put away devices, face the person, make eye contact
2. **Reflect back**: "It sounds like you're saying..." (confirm understanding)
3. **Ask open questions**: "What was that like for you?" not "Were you frustrated?"
4. **Resist fixing**: Sometimes people need to be heard, not solved
5. **Notice non-verbals**: Tone, pace, posture, facial expressions carry meaning

### Perspective-Taking Exercise
Before judging someone's behavior, answer three questions:
1. What might they be dealing with that I cannot see? (personal stress, conflicting priorities)
2. What constraints are they operating under? (deadline, management pressure, limited info)
3. What would I do in their exact situation? (same skills, same information, same pressure)

## Conflict Resolution

### The DESC Model
```
D — Describe the situation objectively (facts, not interpretations)
E — Express how it affects you (use "I" statements)
S — Specify what you want to happen
C — Consequences (positive outcome if resolved)

Example:
D: "In the last three meetings, my suggestions were cut off before I could finish."
E: "I feel undervalued and hesitant to contribute."
S: "I'd like us to adopt a round-robin format where each person finishes their point."
C: "That way everyone's ideas get heard, and we make better decisions."
```

### Conflict Escalation Ladder
| Level | Behavior | Intervention |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| 1. Disagreement | Different opinions, respectful | Normal — encourage healthy debate |
| 2. Friction | Frustration, passive-aggressive signals | Address early in 1-on-1, use DESC |
| 3. Confrontation | Personal attacks, blame, raised voices | Separate, cool down, mediate |
| 4. Entrenchment | Refusal to collaborate, avoidance | Involve manager or neutral mediator |
| 5. Breakdown | Team dysfunction, resignations | Restructure team, executive intervention |

### De-Escalation Techniques
- Lower your voice (mirroring: the other person's volume tends to match yours)
- Acknowledge their perspective first before presenting yours
- Find common ground: "We both want this project to succeed"
- Take a break if emotions are escalating: "Let's revisit this in 30 minutes"

## Stress Management

### Acute Stress Response (In the Moment)
1. **Physiological reset**: 4-7-8 breathing (inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s, repeat 3x)
2. **Cognitive reframe**: "This is challenging" not "This is catastrophic"
3. **Time perspective**: "Will this matter in 1 week? 1 month? 1 year?"
4. **Action focus**: Identify one concrete next step (movement breaks rumination)

### Chronic Stress Prevention
| Strategy | Implementation | Frequency |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Sleep hygiene | 7-9 hours, consistent schedule, no screens 1h before | Daily |
| Physical activity | 30 minutes moderate exercise | 4-5x per week |
| Recovery time | Block non-work time, no email on weekends | Weekly |
| Social connection | Meaningful conversation with someone you trust | 2-3x per week |
| Boundaries | Define work hours and communicate them | Ongoing |

### Burnout Warning Signs
- Emotional exhaustion: dreading work you used to enjoy
- Depersonalization: cynicism about colleagues, users, or the mission
- Reduced efficacy: feeling like nothing you do matters
- Physical symptoms: persistent fatigue, headaches, insomnia
- Action: reduce workload immediately. Burnout does not resolve by pushing through

## Emotional Intelligence in Technical Work

### Code Reviews
- Critique the code, not the coder ("This function could be simplified" not "You wrote bad code")
- Assume positive intent: the author probably had a reason for their approach
- Ask before prescribing: "What led to this approach?" before suggesting alternatives

### Difficult Conversations
- Prepare by writing key points (reduces emotional hijacking)
- Lead with empathy: "I know this sprint has been demanding, and I appreciate your effort"
- Be direct: vague feedback is worse than no feedback
- End with commitment: "What's one thing we can both do differently going forward?"
