# Pricing Strategy

Design pricing that captures value, drives adoption, and maximizes revenue.

## Pricing Models

### Value-Based Pricing
- Price based on the value delivered to the customer, not your costs
- Method: quantify the customer's pain (cost of problem) and price at 10-20% of that
- Example: If your tool saves a company $100K/year, price at $10-20K/year
- Requires deep understanding of customer economics

### Usage-Based Pricing
- Price scales with consumption (API calls, compute hours, seats, transactions)
- Aligns cost with value — customers pay more as they get more
- Include a base fee for predictability + usage charges for upside
- Risk: revenue volatility, harder to forecast

### Tiered / Good-Better-Best
- 3-4 tiers that segment by customer size or needs
- **Free/Starter**: limited features, self-serve, hooks users
- **Professional**: core features, most popular tier (anchor price)
- **Enterprise**: all features, custom, high-touch support
- Price ratios: typically 1x / 3x / 10x across tiers

### Freemium
- Free tier drives adoption and creates product-qualified leads
- Free-to-paid conversion target: 2-5% (consumer), 5-15% (B2B)
- Free tier must be genuinely useful but limited enough to create upgrade desire
- Limit by: usage volume, features, team size, or integrations

## Pricing Psychology

### Anchoring
- Display the most expensive plan first (online) or present it in conversation first
- The highest price becomes the reference point, making lower tiers feel like a deal
- Annual pricing displayed as monthly equivalent ($99/mo billed annually = $1,188/year)

### Charm Pricing
- $49 vs $50 — the left digit effect (9-ending prices feel significantly cheaper)
- Works for consumer and SMB. For enterprise, use round numbers ($10,000)

### Decoy Effect
- Add a plan that's intentionally less attractive to push customers toward the target plan
- Example: Basic $19, Pro $49, Enterprise $99 — if Pro is the target, make Basic feel limiting

## Pricing Experimentation

### A/B Testing Pricing
1. Test on new signups only (never change price on existing customers mid-test)
2. Test one variable at a time: price point, billing frequency, or feature packaging
3. Measure conversion rate, revenue per visitor, and LTV (not just signups)
4. Run for minimum 2 weeks or 200 conversions per variant

### Price Increase Playbook
1. Grandfather existing customers for 6-12 months
2. Add new value before increasing price (new features, integrations)
3. Communicate 60 days before increase with clear justification
4. Expect 2-5% churn — net revenue should still increase

## Common Mistakes

- Pricing based on cost-plus instead of value delivered
- Too many tiers (decision paralysis) — 3-4 tiers is optimal
- Free tier too generous (no upgrade pressure) or too limited (no adoption)
- Never raising prices — leaving revenue on the table
- Not segmenting: charging SMBs and enterprises the same price
